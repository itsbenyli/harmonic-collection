Reflection

A reflection is never still. It ripples at the slightest breeze, reminding me how fragile our vision of ourselves can be. I sit by the water or lean against the glass and watch the world fold into its own image — uncertain, shifting, familiar. To look into a mirror of water or city light is to meet the boundary between what is and what seems.

The surface is calm, but underneath it everything moves — shadows, thoughts, hidden currents. In the lake’s mirror I see the trees upside down, sky and earth reversed. There’s a quiet paradox there: clarity in distortion. The reflection becomes a map of what I feel, not just what I see.

In the city, glass walls and puddles capture neon lights and passing strangers. My silhouette merges with others, fragments of faces and movement combine into one scene. The reflection doesn’t show only me — it shows a moment, an echo, something shared. Every glance becomes a question: who is looking, and who is being reflected?
