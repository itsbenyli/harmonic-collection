I watch myself in fragments—
small moments returning like soft echoes,
carrying pieces of who I used to be,
and the versions of me that never fully disappeared.

Childhood feels like a place I keep trying to revisit,
not through memory alone, but through sensations—
the warmth of a late afternoon,
the uneven rhythm of running without purpose,
the blurry laughter of a smaller world where nothing felt urgent.

Back then, time did not move forward;
it simply gathered around me, gentle and wide.

As I grew older, the edges of that world sharpened.
Rooms became smaller, decisions heavier,
and I learned that silence could carry meaning
instead of simply being the space before the next game.

Yet when I watch these old fragments,
I recognize something steady:
a softness I once lived inside
that I am still trying to understand.

Growing up is not a leaving—it is a layering.
Every version of myself stands somewhere inside me,
still watching, still learning,
still reaching toward the world with open hands.

And maybe the reason these old images feel so alive
is because they remind me
that I did not lose anything at all—
I simply became someone who could carry more.

So I let the film run.
I let the colors fade and return,
let the frame tremble, let the light spill.

In these imperfect recordings,
I see not just who I was,
but the tenderness of becoming—
the quiet proof that even now,
I am still in motion,
still unfolding,
still learning how to be whole.
