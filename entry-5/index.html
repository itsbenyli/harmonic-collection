Small Proofs

I keep small things — a receipt, a ticket, a stone — not because they matter, but because they remind me that moments once did. These objects carry the quiet weight of passing time, fragile evidence that I was here, feeling something worth remembering.

The receipt’s faded ink still shows the name of a café, the date, the small cost of a quiet afternoon. I remember the smell of coffee, the hum of soft conversation. Some moments never grow old; they just turn lighter, like paper slowly surrendering to air and time.

The ticket feels heavier than it looks — not in weight, but in memory. It once promised distance, a train to somewhere unfamiliar. I didn’t know then that leaving could also mean collecting — sounds, faces, fragments that never quite leave you.

A smooth pebble from a nameless shore. I found it when I wasn’t looking for anything. It sits on my desk now, unchanged, patient. Sometimes I hold it just to feel its quiet permanence — a kind of peace that doesn’t need to be explained.
