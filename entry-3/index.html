Window

The window is the quietest part of my room— not because it makes no sound, but because everything beyond the glass feels softened as if distance itself is a kind of mercy.

When I sit in front of it, I feel the world slow down. Light stretches across the walls, shadows drift with the rhythm of passing clouds, and the city becomes a moving painting that expects nothing from me.

Through the window, I can watch life without needing to enter it. I can let my thoughts wander in the safety of observation— half in the world, half tucked inside myself.

When the glass brightens, the dust in the air becomes tiny constellations, suspended in a moment too brief to hold.

Everything looks slightly unreal— like the world is trying on a gentler version of itself. Buildings glow, color deepens, and even familiar streets feel touched by something soft and temporary.

A window doesn’t change anything, yet it changes how I see. It reminds me that perspective is sometimes all I have control over.

Rain turns the window into a moving watercolor— every drop dragging its own story down the glass.

The world outside dissolves, bending into streaks of color and light that shift and gather like emotional weather. Cars blur into lines, people become silhouettes, and the city exhales in long, quiet breaths.

Rain always pulls me inward. It reminds me how easily things change shape— memories, feelings, even the way I imagine myself.

The window holds all of it without ever asking me to explain.
